Pregnancy Journey

Month-by-Month Pregnancy Guide

Comprehensive week-by-week and month-by-month guide to pregnancy — baby development, maternal body changes, recommended tests, nutrition and warning signs.

⚠️
All information is research-based from published obstetric literature. Individual pregnancies vary significantly. All concerns must be discussed with your obstetrician or midwife.
Month 1
🌱

Weeks 1–4

Conception to Implantation

Fertilisation occurs. Zygote travels to uterus and implants (~Day 7-10). hCG begins production — home pregnancy test positive from Day 14+. Neural tube formation begins.

  • Start folic acid 400mcg/day immediately
  • Avoid alcohol, smoking, raw fish, unpasteurised cheese
  • First prenatal appointment: Week 6-8
  • hCG blood test to confirm pregnancy
Month 2
🫘

Weeks 5–8

Embryonic Period — Size: Kidney bean

Heart begins beating (~Week 6). Brain, spinal cord, limb buds forming. All major organs begin development. Morning sickness peaks Weeks 6-8.

  • Dating scan / viability scan: Weeks 6-8
  • Blood group, Rubella immunity, HIV, Hep B tested
  • Ginger, small frequent meals for nausea
  • Rest as needed — fatigue is intense and normal
Month 3
🍤

Weeks 9–12

End of Embryonic Period — Size: Lime

Embryo becomes a foetus at Week 10. Fingers and toes formed. External genitalia forming. NT scan marks end of first trimester screening. Miscarriage risk drops significantly after Week 12.

  • NT (Nuchal Translucency) scan: Weeks 11-14
  • Combined first trimester screening (NT + blood)
  • NIPT (Non-Invasive Prenatal Test) if indicated
  • Morning sickness usually improves after Week 12

First Trimester — Key Information

Warning Signs — Call Your Doctor Immediately

  • Heavy vaginal bleeding (light spotting may be normal — always check)
  • Severe abdominal cramping or one-sided pain (ectopic pregnancy risk)
  • High fever (>38.5°C) — risk of infection
  • Severe vomiting — unable to keep any food/fluid down (Hyperemesis Gravidarum)
  • Passing tissue or large clots — possible miscarriage

Essential Supplements (Always confirm with your doctor)

  • Folic acid 400mcg daily (600mcg if BMI>30 or previous neural tube defect history)
  • Vitamin D 400-1000 IU daily
  • Iron if haemoglobin below 11g/dL (common in Indian women)
  • Iodine 150mcg daily (if not getting adequate from diet)
  • DHA/Omega-3 for foetal brain development
Month 4
🍋

Weeks 13–16

Golden Trimester Begins — Size: Lemon

Energy returns for most women. Baby can move, make facial expressions, hear sounds. Sex can often be determined. Quadruple screen blood test offered.

  • Anomaly scan (Level 2) booked: Weeks 18-20
  • Dental check — pregnancy affects gums
  • Begin pelvic floor exercises (Kegel exercises)
  • Regular moderate exercise generally safe now
Month 5
🥭

Weeks 17–20

Quickening — Size: Mango

Foetal movements felt for first time (quickening) — typically Weeks 18-22. Vernix caseosa (protective coating) appears. Baby develops sleep-wake cycles.

  • Anatomy / Anomaly scan (Level 2 USG): Week 18-20
  • Foetal echo if family history of heart disease
  • Iron and calcium supplementation continued
  • Note first foetal movements — track daily after 28 weeks
Month 6
🌽

Weeks 21–24

Viability — Size: Corn

Viability threshold reached at 24 weeks. Baby develops fingerprints, taste buds, eyelashes. Lungs begin producing surfactant. Braxton Hicks contractions may begin.

  • GDM (Gestational Diabetes) screen: Weeks 24-28
  • Anaemia screen — blood tests
  • Growth scan if concerns
  • Sleep on left side — improves blood flow to placenta
Month 7 (starts)
🥥

Weeks 24–27

Third Trimester Preparation

Baby's eyes open and close. Response to light and sound. Brain development accelerates. Back pain and heartburn become common as uterus expands.

  • Tdap vaccine: Weeks 27-36 (protects newborn from whooping cough)
  • Rhesus negative mothers: Anti-D injection at Week 28
  • Oral Glucose Tolerance Test (OGTT) if screen positive
  • Childbirth classes recommended

Second Trimester — Level 2 Anomaly Scan

The Level 2 (detailed anatomy) scan at 18-20 weeks is the most important scan in pregnancy. It evaluates all major foetal organs:

  • Brain anatomy (cerebellum, ventricles, corpus callosum)
  • Facial features (cleft lip/palate screening), nuchal fold thickness
  • Heart (4-chamber view, outflow tracts — detects most congenital heart defects)
  • Abdominal organs (stomach bubble, liver, kidneys, bladder)
  • Spine (open neural tube defects — spina bifida)
  • Limbs, placental position, umbilical cord vessels, amniotic fluid (AFI)
  • Foetal biometry: BPD (biparietal diameter), HC, AC, FL (femur length) — determines gestational age and growth
Month 7
🍍

Weeks 28–31

Third Trimester — Size: Pineapple

Rapid weight gain. Baby can dream (REM sleep). Fat deposits forming. Brain growth explosive. Foetal movements should be felt daily — kick counting begins.

  • Kick count: 10 movements in 2 hours (from Week 28)
  • Growth scan if IUGR suspected
  • Anti-D injection if Rh negative (Week 28)
  • Prepare birth plan discussion with OB
Month 8
🎃

Weeks 32–35

Final Preparations — Size: Pumpkin

Most organs mature. Baby rotates head-down (vertex). If breech, External Cephalic Version (ECV) considered at 36 weeks. GBS (Group B Strep) swab taken.

  • GBS swab: Week 35-37
  • Weekly antenatal visits begin
  • Growth and wellbeing scan: Week 32-34
  • Hospital bag preparation
Month 9
🎁

Weeks 36–40

Term Pregnancy — Full Term at 39 Weeks

Full term = 39-40 weeks. Lightning (baby drops into pelvis). Cervical changes begin. Braxton Hicks intensify. Mucus plug may pass. Labour expected between Weeks 37-42.

  • Weekly CTG (cardiotocography) if high risk
  • Cervical check from Week 38
  • Post-dates induction discussed at Week 41
  • Signs of labour: regular contractions, water breaking, bloody show

Third Trimester Warning Signs — Seek Emergency Care

  • Severe headache + visual disturbance + swelling — Pre-eclampsia (can be life-threatening)
  • Decreased foetal movement — Less than 10 movements in 2 hours: contact hospital immediately
  • Vaginal bleeding — Any significant bleeding after 20 weeks: placenta praevia, abruption
  • Rupture of membranes (PROM) — Water breaking before 37 weeks: preterm PROM
  • Symptoms of DVT — Unilateral calf pain, swelling, warmth (pregnancy is a hypercoagulable state)
  • Severe epigastric pain — HELLP syndrome, liver involvement
  • Sudden severe shortness of breath — Pulmonary embolism

Labour & Birth — Research Overview

Stages of Labour

  • First Stage — Latent Phase: Cervix dilates 0-6cm. Contractions irregular, 5-20 min apart. May last 8-20 hours in first-time mothers. Rest, warm bath, light snacks advised.
  • First Stage — Active Phase: Cervix dilates 6-10cm. Contractions every 3-5 min lasting 45-60 sec. Hospital admission recommended at this stage.
  • Second Stage — Pushing: From 10cm to birth. Urge to push (Ferguson's reflex). Average 1-2 hours for first-time, 30 min for subsequent births. Supported positions (upright, lateral) associated with better outcomes.
  • Third Stage — Placenta: Delivery of placenta (5-30 min). Active management with oxytocin reduces PPH (postpartum haemorrhage) risk.

Types of Delivery

  • SVD (Spontaneous Vaginal Delivery): Natural birth. Preferred when safe. Faster recovery, better microbiome for baby, immediate skin-to-skin.
  • Assisted Vaginal Delivery: Forceps or vacuum (ventouse) — used for prolonged second stage or foetal distress. Requires episiotomy.
  • Caesarean Section (LSCS): Lower segment CS. Planned (elective) or emergency. Indications: placenta praevia, previous CS (relative), breech, foetal distress, CPD, cord prolapse.
  • VBAC (Vaginal Birth After Caesarean): 60-80% success rate with appropriate selection. Requires informed consent and continuous monitoring.

Pain Relief Options

  • Epidural anaesthesia — Most effective, 80-90% pain relief. Does not increase CS rate when given in active labour.
  • Entonox (gas and air) — 50% nitrous oxide, 50% oxygen. Reduces pain, causes mild sedation.
  • Pethidine/Diamorphine IM — Moderate pain relief. Can cause neonatal respiratory depression if given close to delivery.
  • Spinal anaesthesia — For CS. Rapid onset, excellent coverage.
  • Water birth, breathing techniques, TENS machine — Non-pharmacological options.
Family Planning

Pre-Pregnancy Planning

Optimising health before conception improves fertility, reduces complications, and gives your baby the best start.

Preconception Health — 3-6 Months Before Trying

Medical Checks (Both Partners)

  • Complete blood count, blood group and Rh factor
  • Rubella, Varicella immunity screen — vaccinate if non-immune (then wait 1 month before conceiving)
  • STI screen (HIV, Hep B, Hep C, Syphilis, Gonorrhoea, Chlamydia)
  • Thyroid function tests (TSH) — hypothyroidism strongly linked to miscarriage and infertility
  • Blood sugar / HbA1c — uncontrolled diabetes causes foetal abnormalities
  • Cervical smear (Pap test) if due — not possible during pregnancy
  • Genetic carrier screening (Thalassaemia screen mandatory in India; cystic fibrosis in Caucasians)
  • Male partner: Semen analysis (SA) — 40% of infertility has male factor

Lifestyle Changes

  • Folic acid 400mcg daily: Start at least 3 months before conception. Reduces neural tube defects by 70%.
  • Stop alcohol completely — No safe level in pregnancy. Stop at least 3 months before.
  • Stop smoking — Reduces egg quality, sperm quality, increases miscarriage risk 2x.
  • Achieve healthy BMI (18.5-24.9): Obesity (BMI>30) significantly increases miscarriage, GDM, pre-eclampsia, CS risk. Underweight (BMI<18.5) increases preterm birth, LBW.
  • Avoid extreme exercise — Very low body fat impairs ovulation (hypothalamic amenorrhoea).
  • Review all medications with your doctor — many are teratogenic (harmful to foetus).
  • Dental health — Periodontal disease linked to preterm birth. Complete dental treatment before pregnancy.
  • Stress management — Chronic stress affects HPA axis and reproductive hormones.

Fertility Awareness

  • Ovulation occurs ~14 days before next period (Day 14 in 28-day cycle)
  • Fertile window: 5 days before ovulation + ovulation day (6 days total)
  • Sperm survive 3-5 days; egg survives 12-24 hours
  • LH surge (ovulation predictor kit) occurs 24-36 hours before ovulation
  • Basal Body Temperature (BBT) rises 0.2-0.5°C after ovulation
  • Cervical mucus: clear, stretchy "egg white" consistency indicates fertile phase
  • Average time to conception: 6 months (85% conceive within 1 year of regular unprotected sex)

When to Seek Fertility Help

  • Under 35: Try for 12 months before seeking evaluation
  • 35-40: Seek evaluation after 6 months
  • Over 40: Seek evaluation after 3 months
  • Any age: Seek immediately if known fertility issue (PCOS, previous ectopic, irregular periods, male factor known)
Postnatal Care

Post-Pregnancy & Postnatal Care

Recovery after birth, breastfeeding, postnatal mental health, physical rehabilitation, and newborn care guidance.

Immediate Postnatal Period (0-6 Weeks)

Physical Recovery After Vaginal Birth

  • Perineal pain and healing: Ice packs 24-48hrs, then warm sitz baths, Pelvic floor exercises from Day 1
  • Lochia (postnatal bleeding): Bright red 1-4 days, then pink/brown up to 6 weeks. Foul smell or fever = infection
  • Afterpains: Uterine contractions (especially during breastfeeding) — paracetamol safe
  • Haemorrhoids: Common post-delivery. High fibre diet, stool softeners, topical treatment
  • First bowel movement: May take 2-3 days. Adequate fluid, fibre, gentle movement

Recovery After Caesarean Section

  • Wound care: Keep dry and clean. Dissolvable sutures or staples removed Day 5-7
  • Avoid driving for 6 weeks or until able to perform emergency stop without pain
  • No lifting anything heavier than baby for 6 weeks
  • Deep vein thrombosis (DVT) prophylaxis: LMWH injections (if high risk), compression stockings, early mobilisation
  • Expected recovery: 6-8 weeks to return to normal activities

6-Week Postnatal Check

  • Blood pressure, wound check, anaemia screen
  • Emotional wellbeing assessment (Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale)
  • Contraception counselling — fertility returns before first period
  • Pelvic floor assessment — referral to physiotherapist if incontinence
  • Cervical smear if due (6 weeks after delivery)

Breastfeeding — Evidence-Based Guide

Benefits (WHO recommends exclusive breastfeeding to 6 months)

  • For baby: Optimal nutrition, immunity (IgA, lactoferrin), reduced SIDS risk, reduced obesity, IQ benefit
  • For mother: Faster uterine involution, reduced PPH, lower risk of breast and ovarian cancer, natural contraception (LAM method)

Common Breastfeeding Challenges

  • Latch problems: Most common cause of nipple pain. Wide-open mouth, chin touching breast, asymmetric latch. Seek lactation consultant support.
  • Engorgement: Peaks Day 3-5. Frequent feeding, warm compress before, cold compress after. Never stop feeding abruptly.
  • Mastitis: Breast infection — fever, hard red area, flu symptoms. Continue breastfeeding (milk safe), antibiotics from doctor.
  • Low milk supply: Frequent feeding signals more production. Ensure adequate maternal hydration and calories. Galactagogues: Fenugreek, Shatavari (discuss with doctor).
  • Nipple thrush: Burning pain throughout feed. Both mother and baby treated simultaneously.

Safe Medications During Breastfeeding

  • Generally safe: Paracetamol, Ibuprofen, Amoxicillin, most antihypertensives (but not ACE inhibitors), Metformin
  • Check all medications at LactMed database or with pharmacist/doctor before taking
  • Avoid: Aspirin (Reye syndrome), codeine (sedation), most herbal remedies without expert advice

Postnatal Mental Health

Baby Blues (50-80% of women — Days 3-5)

Transient emotional lability, crying, mood swings caused by rapid oestrogen/progesterone drop after delivery. Self-limiting — resolves by Day 10. Support, rest, reassurance.

Postnatal Depression (10-15% of women)

  • Onset: Any time in first year, most commonly 6-12 weeks post-birth
  • Symptoms: Persistent low mood, inability to bond with baby, loss of pleasure, sleep problems (beyond baby's needs), worthlessness, thoughts of self-harm
  • Treatment: CBT psychotherapy (first line for mild-moderate), SSRIs (sertraline, paroxetine — safe in breastfeeding for moderate-severe), partner/social support
  • Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS) score ≥10: Seek professional evaluation immediately

Postpartum Psychosis (0.1-0.2% — Medical Emergency)

  • Onset: Usually within 2 weeks of birth, often Day 3-5
  • Symptoms: Confusion, hallucinations, delusions, extreme mood swings, inability to sleep
  • EMERGENCY: Requires immediate psychiatric admission. Risk of harm to mother and baby.

Birth Trauma / PTSD

  • Affects ~30% who had difficult births. Flashbacks, avoidance, hypervigilance
  • Trauma-focused CBT and EMDR are evidence-based treatments
  • Debrief with midwife or obstetrician about birth experience if desired

Physical Recovery & Exercise

Pelvic Floor Rehabilitation

  • Start Kegel exercises from Day 1 post-birth (even before stitches heal)
  • Contract pelvic floor for 10 seconds, relax 10 seconds — 10 reps, 3x daily
  • Diastasis Recti (abdominal separation): Present in 60% post-birth. Avoid sit-ups, planks until assessed by physiotherapist
  • Referral to Women's Health Physiotherapist at 6-week check if any prolapse, incontinence, or pelvic pain

Return to Exercise Timeline

  • Week 1-6: Gentle walking, pelvic floor exercises only. No impact exercise
  • Week 6-12: After 6-week check clearance — low impact: swimming, Pilates, cycling
  • Week 12+: Gradual return to jogging, then running (if pelvic floor strong)
  • CS mothers: 8-12 weeks before any core exercise

Postnatal Nutrition

  • Breastfeeding requires extra 500 calories/day above pre-pregnancy intake
  • Iron-rich foods essential — postpartum anaemia very common
  • Calcium: 1000-1300mg/day (breastfeeding leaches calcium from bones)
  • Continue prenatal vitamins for minimum 3 months post-birth if breastfeeding
Reproductive Medicine

Fertility & IVF

Comprehensive research on fertility evaluation, treatment options, IVF protocols, and reproductive technologies.

⚠️
Fertility treatment is highly individualised. All protocols, medications, and decisions must be managed by a qualified reproductive endocrinologist or fertility specialist.

Female Fertility Evaluation

Hormonal Tests (Day 2-3 of cycle)

  • FSH (Follicle Stimulating Hormone): Normal: 3-10 IU/L. Elevated FSH (>10) = diminished ovarian reserve. Very high FSH = poor IVF prognosis.
  • LH (Luteinising Hormone): LH:FSH ratio >2:1 suggests PCOS. LH surge triggers ovulation.
  • AMH (Anti-Müllerian Hormone): Best marker of ovarian reserve. Can be tested any day of cycle. Normal: 1.0-3.5 ng/mL. Low AMH (<1.0) = diminished reserve; very low (<0.5) = poor prognosis.
  • Antral Follicle Count (AFC) on USG: Count of small follicles (2-10mm) at start of cycle. Normal: 8-15 per ovary. AFC <7 total = diminished reserve.
  • Oestradiol (E2), Progesterone: E2 high on Day 2-3 with FSH = poor reserve. Mid-luteal progesterone >30 nmol/L confirms ovulation.
  • TSH, Prolactin, DHEAS, Testosterone: Screen for endocrine causes of infertility.

Structural Investigations

  • Transvaginal Ultrasound (TVUS): Assess uterine morphology, fibroids, polyps, ovarian cysts, AFC, endometrial thickness. First-line investigation.
  • HyCoSy (Hysterosalpingo-Contrast Sonography): Outpatient test for tubal patency using ultrasound contrast. Replaced HSG in many centres.
  • HSG (Hysterosalpingogram): X-ray with contrast dye through fallopian tubes. Detects tubal blockage and uterine abnormalities.
  • Hysteroscopy: Direct visualisation of uterine cavity. Gold standard for intrauterine pathology — fibroids, polyps, adhesions (Asherman's), septum.
  • Laparoscopy: Surgical assessment — endometriosis, pelvic adhesions, tubal disease, ovarian pathology. Gold standard for endometriosis diagnosis.
  • Saline Infusion Sonography (SIS): Saline injected into uterus during ultrasound — defines endometrial polyps and submucosal fibroids.

IVF — In Vitro Fertilisation

Standard Long Protocol (GnRH Agonist)

  • Day 21 previous cycle: Start GnRH agonist (Lupron/Buserelin) — down-regulation/desensitisation of pituitary
  • Day 2-3 of new cycle: Baseline scan to confirm down-regulation. Start FSH/LH stimulation injections (Gonal-F, Menopur, Fostimon). Daily injections for 8-14 days.
  • Monitoring scans: Every 2-3 days to monitor follicle growth. Target: 3+ follicles ≥17mm.
  • Trigger injection: hCG (Ovitrelle) or GnRH agonist when follicles ready. Triggers final maturation — egg collection 36 hours later.
  • Egg Collection (OPU): Transvaginal ultrasound-guided aspiration under sedation. Typically 8-15 eggs retrieved.
  • Fertilisation: Conventional IVF (eggs + sperm incubated) or ICSI (single sperm injected directly into egg).
  • Embryo Development: Monitored Day 1-5/6. Blastocyst (Day 5-6) transfer preferred — higher implantation rates.
  • Embryo Transfer (ET): Fresh transfer Day 3 or Day 5. Or Frozen Embryo Transfer (FET) in subsequent cycle — often better outcome for PCOS, high oestrogen.
  • Luteal Support: Progesterone pessaries/injections after transfer. Continue for 12 weeks if positive test.
  • Pregnancy Test: Beta-hCG blood test 14 days after transfer.

IVF Success Rates (HFEA UK 2022 / ICMR India Data)

  • Under 35: 35-40% live birth rate per cycle
  • 35-37: 25-30% per cycle
  • 38-39: 18-22% per cycle
  • 40-42: 10-15% per cycle
  • 43-44: 5-7% per cycle
  • Over 44: 2-3% per cycle with own eggs; significantly higher with donor eggs

IVF Complications (Research)

  • OHSS (Ovarian Hyperstimulation Syndrome): 1-2% severe. Risk higher in PCOS, young women, high AFC. Signs: Bloating, pain, shortness of breath, reduced urination. Managed by freeze-all strategy.
  • Multiple pregnancy risk (reduced with single embryo transfer — now standard)
  • Egg collection complications: Bleeding, infection (<0.5%)

Other Assisted Reproductive Technologies

IUI — Intrauterine Insemination

  • Washed sperm placed directly into uterine cavity at time of ovulation
  • Success rate: 10-20% per cycle (higher with mild stimulation)
  • Indications: Unexplained infertility, mild male factor, donor sperm, cervical mucus hostility
  • Not recommended: Bilateral tubal blockage, severe male factor, poor ovarian reserve

Egg Freezing (Social / Medical)

  • Eggs frozen at peak fertility for use later (social egg freezing)
  • Vitrification (flash-freezing) — 90%+ survival rate post-thaw
  • Best before age 35 (ideal: under 32). After 37, significantly fewer mature eggs retrieved.
  • Medical: Before chemotherapy/radiotherapy (fertility preservation)

Donor Conception

  • Egg donation: For poor ovarian reserve, premature ovarian failure, recurrent IVF failure, genetic conditions, advanced age
  • Sperm donation: Azoospermia (no sperm), severe male factor, genetic disease carrier, same-sex couples
  • In India: ART Act 2021 and Surrogacy Act 2021 regulate all donor programmes
  • Embryo donation: From couples who have completed family and donated spare embryos

Surgical Sperm Retrieval

  • TESA (Testicular Sperm Aspiration) — Fine needle aspiration of testis
  • TESE (Testicular Sperm Extraction) — Surgical biopsy for azoospermia
  • Micro-TESE — Microsurgical exploration — best results in non-obstructive azoospermia

Preimplantation Genetic Testing (PGT)

  • PGT-A (Aneuploidies): Screens all chromosomes — selects euploid embryos. Reduces miscarriage, improves implantation especially in older women.
  • PGT-M (Monogenic): Tests for specific genetic conditions (BRCA, Thalassaemia, CF) — selects unaffected embryos
  • PGT-SR (Structural Rearrangements): For chromosomal translocations in parents

Clinical Research

Gynaecological Conditions

AI-researched clinical information on common and complex gynaecological conditions from peer-reviewed literature and clinical guidelines.

⚠️
All information is for educational research. Diagnosis requires clinical examination and investigation by a qualified gynaecologist. Do not self-diagnose.

Quick Reference — Common Conditions

PCOS — Key Facts

  • Affects 8-13% of reproductive-age women worldwide
  • Rotterdam Criteria (2 of 3): Irregular periods, hyperandrogenism, polycystic ovaries on USG
  • Management: Lifestyle first (5-10% weight loss restores ovulation in 80% of obese PCOS). Metformin, COCP, Letrozole for ovulation induction. Inositol supplementation (evidence-based)
  • Long-term risks: T2DM (5x higher risk), endometrial cancer (if anovulation untreated), cardiovascular disease, sleep apnoea

Endometriosis — Key Facts

  • Affects 10-15% of women of reproductive age; 30-40% with infertility
  • Diagnosis: ONLY by laparoscopy with biopsy. Clinical/USG diagnosis is presumptive only.
  • Management: Hormonal (COCP, progestogens, GnRH analogues), Surgery (laparoscopic excision — gold standard), IVF for associated infertility
  • Average diagnostic delay: 7-12 years. Advocate for yourself if dysmenorrhoea is severe.

Fibroids — Key Facts

  • Benign uterine smooth muscle tumours. Present in 20-40% of women over 35.
  • Types: Submucosal (inside cavity — cause heavy bleeding, infertility), Intramural (within wall), Subserosal (outside), Pedunculated (on stalk)
  • Treatment: Medical (tranexamic acid, Mirena IUS, GnRH analogues for shrinkage), Surgical (myomectomy — preserve fertility, or hysterectomy), UAE (Uterine Artery Embolisation)

Cervical Cancer Prevention

  • HPV (Human Papillomavirus) causes 99.7% of cervical cancers. Types 16 and 18 cause 70%.
  • HPV vaccine (Gardasil 9): 9-14 years for best response. Up to age 45 still beneficial.
  • Cervical screening (Pap smear / HPV test): Every 3-5 years from age 25 (varies by country)
  • India: Cervical cancer 2nd most common female cancer. HPV vaccine recently added to national programme (2023)
  • Colposcopy + biopsy if abnormal smear. Treatment: LLETZ, cone biopsy for CIN
Clinical Management

Nutritional Science

Diet & Nutrition

Evidence-based nutritional guidance for every stage of a woman's reproductive health journey.

Pregnancy Nutrition — Trimester-Specific

First Trimester (Calories: +0-100 kcal/day)

  • Folic acid: Leafy greens (spinach, methi, palak), fortified cereals, lentils. Supplement 400mcg/day.
  • For nausea: Ginger tea, small frequent meals every 2 hours, cold foods (less smell), crackers before rising, vitamin B6 25mg 3x/day
  • Iron: Dal, leafy greens, jaggery, ragi, pomegranate, amla (Vitamin C enhances absorption). Avoid tea/coffee within 1 hour of iron-rich food.
  • Foods to avoid completely: Raw/undercooked meat and eggs, unpasteurised milk and cheese, raw papaya, pineapple, excess Vitamin A (liver), alcohol, caffeine >200mg/day

Second Trimester (Calories: +300 kcal/day)

  • Calcium: 1000mg/day. Milk, curd, paneer, ragi, sesame seeds, almonds. Baby's skeleton actively building.
  • DHA/Omega-3: Fatty fish (if non-vegetarian), walnuts, flaxseeds, algae-based DHA supplement for vegetarians. Foetal brain development peak.
  • Protein: 1.1-1.3g/kg/day. Dal, rajma, chana, paneer, eggs, chicken, fish, tofu.
  • Constipation prevention: High fibre (whole grains, fruit, vegetables), 2.5-3 litres water/day

Third Trimester (Calories: +450 kcal/day)

  • Continue all above. Rapid foetal growth — nutrient demand highest.
  • Heartburn management: Small frequent meals, avoid lying down after eating, avoid spicy/fried/citrus foods
  • GDM diet (if diagnosed): Low glycaemic index foods, portion control carbohydrates, increase protein and fat proportion
  • Vitamin K: Leafy greens — important for neonatal coagulation (also given as injection to newborn)

Fertility Diet — Evidence-Based

The Mediterranean Diet pattern has the strongest evidence for fertility improvement (Human Reproduction 2019 meta-analysis). Both male and female fertility benefit.

Fertility-Boosting Foods (Research-Supported)

  • Antioxidants reduce oxidative stress damage to eggs and sperm: Berries, pomegranate, dark chocolate (70%+), green tea, leafy greens
  • Healthy fats: Avocado, olive oil, walnuts, fatty fish (omega-3). Associated with improved endometrial receptivity and egg quality.
  • Plant protein: Replacing animal protein with vegetable protein associated with lower ovulatory infertility risk (Chavarro, NEJM 2007)
  • Full-fat dairy (1 portion/day): The Nurses' Health Study found 1 serving of full-fat dairy associated with lower ovulatory infertility risk vs low-fat dairy
  • CoQ10 (Coenzyme Q10): 600mg/day improves egg quality and sperm motility/morphology (multiple RCTs). Especially beneficial for women over 35.
  • Inositol (Myo-Inositol 2g + D-chiro-inositol 50mg 2x/day): Strong evidence for PCOS — improves ovulation, egg quality, reduces insulin resistance

Fertility Diet Avoid List

  • Trans fats: 2% increase in trans fat calories = 73% increase in ovulatory infertility risk (Nurses' Health Study)
  • Alcohol: >2 units/day reduces IVF success rates by 13-24%
  • High-mercury fish: Shark, swordfish, king mackerel, tilefish — mercury accumulates in eggs
  • Processed meats, excess red meat: Associated with endometriosis progression
  • High glycaemic index foods: Rapid glucose spikes worsen insulin resistance — impairs ovulation in PCOS

PCOS Diet — Evidence-Based Management

Insulin resistance is present in 65-70% of women with PCOS. Dietary management of insulin resistance is the cornerstone of PCOS treatment.

PCOS-Specific Dietary Principles

  • Low Glycaemic Index (GI) Diet: Replaces high-GI carbs with low-GI alternatives. Reduces insulin spikes. Oats, bajra, jowar, dalia, brown rice, legumes over white rice, bread, refined flour.
  • Anti-inflammatory diet: PCOS is an inflammatory condition. Turmeric, ginger, omega-3, antioxidants, and avoiding refined sugar all reduce chronic inflammation.
  • Protein at every meal: Slows glucose absorption, maintains satiety, supports muscle mass (important for insulin sensitivity).
  • Fibre (25-30g/day): Psyllium husk, oats, legumes, vegetables — improves insulin sensitivity, reduces androgen absorption.
  • 5-10% weight loss: In overweight/obese PCOS — restores ovulation in 80%, reduces androgens, improves insulin sensitivity.

Specific Foods for PCOS

  • Spearmint tea (2 cups/day): Reduces free testosterone — mild anti-androgenic effect (RCT evidence)
  • Cinnamon (1/2 tsp/day in food): Improves insulin sensitivity, may regulate menstrual cycles in PCOS
  • Apple cider vinegar (1 tbsp in water before meals): May improve insulin sensitivity (small trials)
  • Flaxseeds (1-2 tbsp/day): Lignans reduce androgen activity, high fibre
  • Berberine: Insulin sensitiser — comparable to Metformin in some studies

Postpartum Nutrition

Key Nutritional Needs

  • Iron: Most important. Post-birth blood loss + pregnancy depletion = almost universal postpartum anaemia. Red meat (2-3x/week if non-vegetarian), spinach + Vitamin C, iron supplements if Hb <10.
  • Calcium: 1300mg/day while breastfeeding (higher than pregnancy). Breastfeeding leaches calcium from maternal bones. Milk, curd, ragi, sesame, almonds.
  • Extra calories (breastfeeding): Additional 500 kcal/day. Do not restrict calories while breastfeeding — affects milk supply and quality.
  • Hydration: 3+ litres/day while breastfeeding. Milk production requires adequate hydration.
  • Traditional Indian postpartum foods (evidence-supported benefits):
  • Methi (fenugreek) laddoos — galactagogue, iron, fibre
  • Dink (edible gum) laddoos — bone strengthening, energy
  • Ajwain (carom seeds) water — digestive, anti-inflammatory, uterine involution
  • Dry fruits and nuts — iron, healthy fats, energy density
  • Panjiri — traditional postnatal blend with ghee, whole wheat, nuts, dry fruits

Postpartum Weight Loss

  • Breastfeeding burns 300-500 calories/day — assists gradual weight loss
  • Target 0.5-1kg loss per week maximum while breastfeeding
  • Do not start calorie-restricted diet before 6-8 weeks postpartum
  • Most women return to pre-pregnancy weight by 6-12 months with balanced diet and activity

Exercise & Yoga

Evidence-based exercise guidance for every stage of women's health — pregnancy, postpartum, fertility, PCOS, and menopause.

Exercise in Pregnancy (ACOG/RCOG Guidelines)

150 minutes of moderate intensity aerobic exercise per week is recommended throughout uncomplicated pregnancy. Exercise reduces GDM risk by 38%, pre-eclampsia by 41%, excessive weight gain, back pain, and postpartum depression.

Safe Exercises During Pregnancy

  • Walking — ideal throughout pregnancy; 30 minutes daily
  • Swimming and aqua aerobics — low impact, excellent all trimesters
  • Prenatal yoga — improves flexibility, reduces anxiety, pelvic floor strengthening
  • Cycling (stationary bike) — safe until balance becomes difficult (3rd trimester)
  • Low-impact aerobics, Pilates (modified)
  • Strength training with lighter weights, correct form, avoid Valsalva manoeuvre

Exercises to Avoid in Pregnancy

  • Contact sports — risk of abdominal trauma (boxing, martial arts, rugby)
  • Supine (lying on back) exercise after 20 weeks — aortocaval compression
  • High-altitude training, scuba diving (foetal risk)
  • High-fall risk activities (skiing, horse riding, gymnastics)
  • Hot yoga / hot tub — hyperthermia risk to foetus (core temp must stay below 39°C)
  • Stop if: Chest pain, shortness of breath, dizziness, vaginal bleeding, severe pain, decreased foetal movement

Kegel / Pelvic Floor Exercises (Throughout Pregnancy and After)

  • Contract pelvic floor (as if stopping urine flow) for 5-10 seconds, relax 10 sec. 10 reps, 3 times daily.
  • Prevent and treat stress urinary incontinence, prolapse risk, improve sexual function
  • Start in first trimester, continue postpartum indefinitely

Prenatal Yoga Sequence

  • Cat-Cow (Marjaryasana-Bitilasana): Relieves back pain, improves spinal mobility. Safe all trimesters.
  • Warrior II (Virabhadrasana II): Strengthens legs and hips, builds stamina. Avoid if pelvic girdle pain.
  • Butterfly Pose (Baddha Konasana): Opens hips, stretches inner thighs. Excellent preparation for labour.
  • Supported Squat (Malasana): Opens pelvis, strengthens pelvic floor, encourages optimal foetal positioning. Use support/wall.
  • Child's Pose (Balasana): Wide-knee version accommodates bump. Relieves back pain, promotes relaxation.
  • Side-lying Savasana: Final relaxation — left side with pillow support between knees and under abdomen.
  • Pranayama: Nadi Shodhana (alternate nostril) — reduces anxiety, balances nervous system. Avoid Kapalabhati, Bhastrika in pregnancy.

Exercise for PCOS & Fertility

  • 150+ minutes moderate exercise/week — reduces insulin resistance, restores ovulation, reduces androgens
  • Combination of aerobic (walking, swimming, cycling) and resistance training most effective for PCOS
  • Even 2.5 hours of vigorous exercise/week reduces risk of ovulatory infertility by 5% (Nurses' Health Study)
  • Excessive exercise (elite athletes, marathon runners) can suppress ovulation via hypothalamic-pituitary axis
  • Body weight considerations: 5-10% weight loss in overweight PCOS restores ovulation in majority
Midlife Women's Health

Menopause & Perimenopause

Research-based guide to the menopausal transition — symptoms, hormone therapy, bone health, cardiovascular risk, and natural management.

The Menopausal Transition

Definitions

  • Perimenopause: 2-10 years before final menstrual period. Irregular cycles, early symptoms.
  • Menopause: 12 consecutive months without menstruation. Average age: 51 (range 45-55). India: average 47.5 years.
  • Premature Ovarian Insufficiency (POI): Menopause before age 40. Affects 1% of women. Requires HRT until natural menopause age.
  • Postmenopause: From 12 months after final period onwards.

Common Symptoms (Oestrogen Deficiency)

  • Vasomotor: Hot flushes (73% of women), night sweats, palpitations
  • Genitourinary Syndrome (GSM): Vaginal dryness, dyspareunia (painful sex), urinary urgency/frequency, UTI frequency
  • Psychological: Mood changes, anxiety, brain fog, poor concentration, low libido
  • Sleep: Insomnia — often secondary to night sweats
  • Musculoskeletal: Joint pains, muscle aches, accelerated bone loss (osteoporosis)
  • Skin: Collagen loss → thinning, wrinkles; hair thinning

Hormone Replacement Therapy (HRT/MHT)

The 2022 re-evaluation of HRT evidence (NICE, British Menopause Society, IMS) strongly supports HRT as the most effective treatment for menopausal symptoms with acceptable risk profile for most healthy women under 60 or within 10 years of menopause.

Types of HRT

  • Combined HRT (oestrogen + progestogen): For women with a uterus. Progestogen protects endometrium. Types: Sequential (bleed) or continuous (no bleed)
  • Oestrogen-only HRT: For women post-hysterectomy only. Slightly better safety profile than combined.
  • Routes: Transdermal (patch, gel, spray) — lower VTE and stroke risk than oral. Oral — convenient. Vaginal (local) — for GSM symptoms only, minimal systemic absorption.
  • Body-identical/bioidentical HRT: Oestradiol + micronised progesterone (Utrogestan) — regulated bioidentical HRT with best safety evidence

Benefits of HRT (Research Summary)

  • Most effective treatment for vasomotor symptoms and GSM
  • Prevents osteoporosis — reduces fracture risk by 30-40%
  • Reduces all-cause mortality when started before 60 (WHI re-analysis 2022)
  • Cardioprotective when started within 10 years of menopause (timing hypothesis)
  • Reduces risk of colorectal cancer, Type 2 diabetes

Non-Hormonal Options (For those who cannot take HRT)

  • Fezolinetant (Veozah): First non-hormonal, non-SSRI prescription treatment for hot flushes (FDA approved 2023)
  • SSRIs/SNRIs: Venlafaxine, Escitalopram — reduce hot flush frequency 40-60%
  • Gabapentin: Effective for night sweats and hot flushes
  • Vaginal oestrogen: Very low systemic absorption — safe even for breast cancer survivors (BMS guideline 2023)
  • Phytoestrogens: Soy isoflavones, black cohosh, red clover — modest benefit. Quality varies by product.

Bone Health in Menopause

  • DEXA scan: Bone density measurement. T-score: Normal ≥-1.0; Osteopaenia -1.0 to -2.5; Osteoporosis ≤-2.5
  • Calcium: 1200mg/day from food and supplement (carbonate with food, citrate on empty stomach)
  • Vitamin D: 1000-2000 IU/day. Essential for calcium absorption. Most Indian women deficient.
  • Weight-bearing exercise (walking, dancing) and resistance training — maintain bone density
  • Bisphosphonates (Alendronate, Zoledronate): If DEXA confirms osteoporosis or high fracture risk
